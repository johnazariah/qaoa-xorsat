## 1. The Question

Stephen wrote:

> People often assert that the universal curves in QAOA can be interpreted
> as large-timestep Trotterizations of adiabatic optimization.  […]  Eddie
> and I are both skeptical.

Our Q1 work supplies decisive numerical evidence on D-regular MaxCut on
the infinite-girth tree: optimal QAOA schedules are **not** consistent
with a Trotterisation of linear adiabatic optimisation.  This document
examines how that evidence bears on the two papers Stephen identified.

The two papers represent opposite ends of the literature.  Paper 1
(SGIR-QAOA, Apr 2026) is the **strawman version**: it explicitly asserts
"QAOA is a Trotterised discretisation of the QAA" and constructs its
method on top of that premise.  Paper 2 (Wurtz–Love, 2021) is the
**sophisticated version**: it agrees that the simple Trotterised-adiabatic
picture is inadequate and proposes a counterdiabatic refinement instead.
Both must be addressed, but in different ways.

## 2. Paper 1 — SGIR-QAOA (McDowall, Georgopoulos, Wallden, Apr 2026)

### 2.1 What they claim

The paper proposes **Spectral Gap Informed Ramp QAOA** (SGIR-QAOA), a
de-variationalised QAOA where the angles are picked from a smooth ramp
that slows down where the spectral gap of $H_\text{ad}(s) = (1-s)H_X +
s H_C$ is small.  The motivation is built directly on the assertion
(§III, paraphrased):

> Given that QAOA is a Trotterized discretisation of the QAA — applying
> alternating unitaries generated by a mixer Hamiltonian and a cost
> Hamiltonian — we set the initial adiabatic Hamiltonian $H_0 = H_\text{mix}$.

The baseline they improve upon is **Linear Ramp QAOA (LR-QAOA)** of
[Montanez-Barrera & Michielsen, npj QI 2025], which uses

$$
\beta_i = \left(1 - \tfrac{i}{p}\right)\,\Delta\beta, \qquad
\gamma_i = \tfrac{i+1}{p}\,\Delta\gamma, \qquad i=0,\ldots,p-1.
\tag{1}
$$

This is precisely our "linear adiabatic" schedule in E1, up to the
tiny $1/p$ shift.

The paper's experiments are on Grover's problem and Maximum Independent
Set (MIS), not on D-regular MaxCut — so it does not directly contradict
our results.  But the underlying premise (Eq. 1 is a sensible
parametrisation because QAOA is Trotterised adiabatic) is testable on
D-regular MaxCut, and that is exactly what E1 of our spec measures.

### 2.2 What our Q1 results say

| Claim | Our evidence | Verdict |
|-------|--------------|---------|
| Eq. (1) is a sensible variational ansatz on D-regular MaxCut | E1: relative loss $\geq 0.86$ for $D \geq 5$ at matched magnitudes | **fails** |
| The optimal QAOA schedule has the *shape* of a linear ramp | E4: cubic fit recovers $\geq 0.04$ extra $r^2$ at every $D$; D=5 $\beta$ is bang-bang | **fails** |
| The optimal depth-$p$ schedule extends adiabatically as $p$ grows | E2: depth-$p$ optimum truncated to depth $t$ is far from depth-$t$ optimum | **fails** |
| Linear-adi seeds reach the QAOA basin under L-BFGS | E3: best-of-12 grid lands $\Delta = 0.004 \ldots 0.013$ in $\tilde c$ below warm-start at every $D$ | **fails** |

The strongest piece of evidence against Paper 1's premise on D-regular
MaxCut is **E1+E3 jointly**: the linear-adi schedule is not just
suboptimal in value (E1), it is in a different basin from the global
QAOA optimum (E3).

### 2.3 What this does not say

We do **not** claim that SGIR-QAOA is wrong on Grover or MIS.  Their
empirical results — that smoothing the ramp via the spectral gap of
$H_\text{ad}$ improves over linear ramps — may well hold on those
problems.  Grover's problem has a single isolated minimum gap and a
huge structural symmetry; MIS with the heavy penalty term they use
($\lambda=2000$) likewise has dramatic gap closings near $s=1$.  In
those settings the spectral-gap-shaped envelope captures real physics.

Our claim is narrower and more specific: on D-regular MaxCut on the
infinite-girth tree, the **premise** that the optimal QAOA angles are
adiabatic-shaped is empirically false.  This calls into question the
generality of using LR-QAOA / SGIR-QAOA as warm starts for QAOA
parameter search outside the Grover/MIS-style regime.

## 3. Paper 2 — CD-QAOA (Wurtz & Love, 2021)

This is the sophisticated paper, and Stephen rightly flagged it as
needing "a critical eye."

### 3.1 What they actually claim

Wurtz and Love do **not** claim that QAOA is a Trotterised adiabatic
schedule.  Their actual claim is more subtle, and in places it agrees
with us.  The key passages:

> Matching QAOA with a simple Trotterized adiabatic path is inadequate
> to describe the effective evolution, and these extra terms [the
> counterdiabatic auxiliary field $s(t)$] are required.

> An optimal protocol for a given $p$ may be highly non-adiabatic, with
> the intermediate state being far from an instantaneous ground state
> and angles being highly nontrivial.  It may be an interesting future
> study to find when optimal QAOA angles are smooth, or non-smooth.

So Wurtz–Love's own caveats encompass exactly what we observe.  Their
claim is **not** "linear adiabatic ≈ optimal QAOA."  It is the chain
of inequalities (§8 of their paper):

$$
\tilde c\bigl[\text{Optimal QAOA, fixed } T\bigr]
\geq \tilde c\bigl[\text{Optimal CD-QAOA, fixed } T\bigr]
\geq \tilde c\bigl[\text{CD-QAOA with fixed } T,p\bigr]
\stackrel{\text{large } p}{=}
\tilde c\bigl[\text{Counterdiabatic, fixed } T\bigr]
\geq \tilde c\bigl[\text{Adiabatic, fixed } T\bigr]
$$

with "angle budget" $T = \sum_q (|\gamma_q| + |\beta_q|)$.  Their core
positive claim is that the second-order BCH commutator term in the
effective QAOA Hamiltonian

$$
H_\text{eff} = \tfrac{\gamma}{\gamma+\beta}H_T + \tfrac{\beta}{\gamma+\beta}H_S
- i \tfrac{\gamma\beta}{\gamma+\beta}\bigl[H_T,H_S\bigr]
$$

is **always negative** with the same sign as the lowest-order
variational adiabatic gauge potential (AGP) — meaning the Trotter
"error" is *helpful*, not detrimental, and so QAOA is "at least
counterdiabatic, not just adiabatic."

For D-regular MaxCut on a graph of degree $\nu$ with no triangles,
they derive (their Eq. 45):

$$
\alpha(\lambda) = -\frac{32(1-\lambda)^2 - 8(3\nu-2)\lambda^2}
{256\bigl((1-\lambda)^2 + 4(3\nu-2)\lambda^2\bigr)^2 + 256\lambda^2(1-\lambda)^2(\nu-1) + 96(\nu-1)(\nu-2)\lambda^4}.
$$

This $\alpha(\lambda)$ encodes the magnitude of the counterdiabatic
correction; matching it to the BCH term fixes a specific non-linear
schedule for $(\gamma_q, \beta_q)$.  Wurtz–Love's headline empirical
result is on a 14-vertex 3-regular MaxCut graph at $p=12$, where the
variationally optimised CD-QAOA angles achieve approximation ratio
$C_{12} = 0.99004$.

### 3.2 The asymptotic claim (their Eq. 3)

Wurtz–Love claim that under Kibble–Zurek scaling, CD-QAOA achieves

$$
1 - C(p) \sim p^{-\mu}.
$$

But — critically — they then add (paraphrasing their §1):

> Such scaling may be slower than optimal QAOA angles for a given $p$.
> We show in §6 that $C(p) \sim 1 - p^{-1/2}$ for the Ising chain and
> finite-speed protocols.  For optimal protocols, we show
> $C(p) \sim 1 - p^{-1}\log p$.  However, the optimal QAOA angles
> [Farhi et al. 2014] go as $C(p) = 1 - 1/(2p)$.  This scaling
> suggests that **QAOA angles which mimic counterdiabatic evolution
> may not be optimal in all systems, and globally optimal protocols
> may be hard to find.**

So Wurtz–Love themselves explicitly acknowledge that CD-QAOA is **not
provably the global optimum**.  Their construction is a *constructive
lower bound* on QAOA performance, not a characterisation of where the
global optimum lives.

### 3.3 What our Q1 evidence says about CD-QAOA

The honest answer is: our experiments do **not** directly test
CD-QAOA.  We tested *linear* adiabatic (the LR-QAOA / SGIR-QAOA
strawman), not the specific non-linear CD-QAOA schedule that
Wurtz–Love construct.  Strict logic:

- E1 fails for **linear** adi.  CD-QAOA is a non-linear refinement.
  Wurtz–Love would not be surprised.
- E2 (truncation) is more interesting: the depth-$p$ optimum is not a
  prefix of the depth-($p+k$) optimum.  Wurtz–Love claim (their §7 +
  Fig. 6) that the induced continuous protocol $\lambda(t), s(t)$
  derived from the depth-$p$ optimum should yield a similar continuous
  protocol that *can* be re-discretised to depth $p'$ with similar
  performance.  Our truncation is not their procedure — they
  re-discretise via the inverse of Eq. 29, we just chop the angle
  array.  So E2 does not refute CD-QAOA's interpolation claim.
- E3 (adi-init) is similar: we seed with **linear** adi, not with the
  CD-QAOA prescription.  L-BFGS landing in a worse basin means linear
  adiabatic seeds are bad seeds; it is silent on whether CD-QAOA
  seeds would be better.
- E4 (curvature) is the cleanest engagement.  Wurtz–Love predict
  smooth optimal angles for graphs with smooth adiabatic gauge
  potentials.  We find:
  - $D \in \{4, 6, 7, 8\}$: $r^2(\text{linear}) \in [0.89, 0.98]$,
    consistent with smooth profiles, but cubic always picks up
    $\geq 0.02$ extra $r^2$ — not a proof of CD-QAOA's prediction
    failing, but tighter than they tested.
  - $D = 5$, $\beta$: $r^2(\text{linear}) = 0.02$, the optimal
    profile zigzags between $\sim 1.1$ and $\sim 2.1$ from step to
    step.  This is **bang-bang** and was anticipated by Wurtz–Love's
    own caveat ("an optimal protocol for a given $p$ may be highly
    non-adiabatic").
  - $D = 3$, $\beta$: $r^2(\text{linear}) = 0.15$, nearly as bad.

So E4 is the experiment that comes closest to engaging Wurtz–Love
seriously.  Even there, it is not a clean refutation: their §7
discussion uses a 14-vertex *finite* graph, while we use the
infinite-girth tree.  The difference may matter (the finite graph has
real spectral gap closings that the tree does not have).

### 3.4 The cleanest experiment we could run to address Wurtz–Love directly

**Compute the Wurtz–Love CD-QAOA prescription on the infinite-girth
tree, evaluate it via our pipeline, and compare to our warm-start
optimum.**  Their Eq. (45) gives $\alpha(\lambda)$ for $\nu$-regular
graphs with no triangles — the infinite-girth tree satisfies that
condition trivially.  The forward procedure (their §4) takes a
counterdiabatic continuous protocol and maps it to QAOA angles via a
BCH expansion; we could run their map at $\nu \in \{3,\ldots,8\}$,
$p \in \{1,\ldots,12\}$, evaluate $\tilde c$ in our pipeline, and
plot it against our warm-start curve.  This would be the *direct*
test:

- If CD-QAOA tracks the warm-start curve closely, Wurtz–Love is
  vindicated on D-regular MaxCut on the tree, and our message
  weakens to "the *strawman* version of QAOA-as-Trotterised-adiabatic
  fails, but the CD refinement holds."
- If CD-QAOA falls visibly short of the warm-start curve at every
  $(D, p)$ — and especially if the gap widens with $D$ or $p$ —
  Wurtz–Love's quantitative claim that CD-QAOA captures most of the
  optimum is contradicted on this problem class.

This is a tractable extension and would close the loop on Stephen's
question.  It belongs in a follow-up Q1.5 spec.

## 4. Synthesis

### 4.1 What Q1 establishes (cleanly)

1. **The strawman version is dead** on D-regular MaxCut on the
   infinite-girth tree.  Linear adiabatic schedules, even with
   magnitudes matched to the QAOA endpoint, do not approximate the
   QAOA optimum and are not in the QAOA optimum's basin.  This
   directly invalidates the premise on which Paper 1 (SGIR-QAOA)
   builds, in our problem regime.
2. **The "$p$-extension is adiabatic continuation" intuition is
   broken.**  Truncating the depth-$p$ optimum to depth $t$ does not
   recover anything close to the depth-$t$ optimum.  Whatever the
   depth-$p$ optimum is, it isn't an adiabatic ramp that one extends
   by adding more time at the end.
3. **The optimal angle profiles are visibly non-smooth at some
   $D$.**  In particular, $D=5$ at $p=12$ shows step-to-step
   bang-bang oscillation in $\beta$, anticipated by Pontryagin's
   minimum principle (which Wurtz–Love themselves cite).

### 4.2 What Q1 does *not* establish (yet)

1. We have not directly tested the Wurtz–Love CD-QAOA construction.
   That requires implementing their Eq. (29)/(45) on the
   infinite-girth tree and comparing.
2. We have not measured the "fidelity" of intermediate QAOA states
   with adiabatic-path ground states (Stephen's first concrete
   suggestion).  That is harder for us because we work on the
   infinite tree where there is no global state — only marginals.
   On a finite-instance simulator we could measure
   $|\langle \psi_q^\text{QAOA} | \psi^\text{ground}(s_q)\rangle|^2$
   directly.
3. We have not tested at $p \to \infty$.  Wurtz–Love's claim is
   asymptotic, ours is at $p \leq 12$ (D-dependent).  The scaling of
   the gap between warm-start and adiabatic-init with $p$ would be
   informative.

### 4.3 Recommendation for the paper

Frame the contribution as: **"We provide the first direct numerical
test of the QAOA-is-Trotterised-adiabatic hypothesis on D-regular
MaxCut, and find it fails by a wide margin in three independent
ways."**

Cite both papers.  Cite Paper 1 as the type of work that motivates
the question (it builds on the failed premise).  Cite Paper 2
respectfully — they were honest about the caveats and our results
align with their own caveat ("optimal QAOA angles may be highly
non-adiabatic") — but note that the constructive CD-QAOA story they
tell is not our story.
