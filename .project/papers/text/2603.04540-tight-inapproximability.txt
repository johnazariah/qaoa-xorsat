We establish tight inapproximability bounds for max-LINSAT, the problem
of maximizing the number of satisfied linear constraints over the finite field Fq ,
where each constraint accepts r values. Specifically, we prove by a direct reduction from Håstad’s theorem that no polynomial-time algorithm can exceed
the random-assignment ratio r/q by any constant, assuming P ̸= NP. This
threshold coincides with the ℓ/m → 0 limit of the semicircle law governing
decoded quantum interferometry (DQI), where ℓ is the decoding radius of the
underlying code: as the decodable structure vanishes, DQI’s approximation
ratio degrades to exactly the worst-case bound established by our result. Together, these observations delineate the boundary between worst-case hardness
and potential quantum advantage, showing that any algorithm surpassing r/q
must exploit algebraic structure specific to the instance.

1 Introduction
Recent years have seen growing interest in quantum algorithms for combinatorial optimization [1], particularly for problems with strong worst-case classical hardness. Given the
practical and industrial relevance of these problems across a wide range of scheduling and
routing tasks, the design of corresponding quantum algorithms is of considerable importance. For such problems, one cannot expect a quantum computer to solve or approximate
all instances of the combinatorial optimization problem in polynomial time. This does not
mean that one cannot hope that quantum computers will have computational advantages
over classical computers. Indeed, in such cases, potential super-polynomial quantum advantages are often investigated on restricted or structured families of instances [2–5]. One
such example that has recently been given a lot of attention is the decoded quantum interferometry (DQI) algorithm introduced in Ref. [6]. DQI works by using the quantum
Fourier transform to reduce the optimization problem to a decoding task, and then coherently applies an efficient decoding algorithm to amplify the quantum interference between
satisfying assignments. The approximation ratio of DQI is characterized by a closed-form
formula, coined the semicircle law [6].
DQI targets a generalized linear constraint satisfaction problem over finite fields, commonly referred to as max-LINSAT. The flagship application is optimal polynomial intersection (OPI) problem, a structured max-LINSAT instance whose constraint matrix has

up to their designed error radius. For OPI, in a certain parameter regime, DQI achieves
a super-polynomial speed-up over known classical algorithms.
A growing body of follow-up work has developed around DQI [7–24]. Most relevant to
our setting, the DQI advantage for OPI has been strengthened via soft decoding of Reed–
Solomon codes [7, 22] and extended to algebraic geometry codes via a generalization called
HOPI [17]. At the same time, several works have exposed DQI’s limitations [13, 14, 16].
A prerequisite for precisely interpreting the results surrounding DQI is a clear understanding of the approximability limits of the underlying optimization problem. For
many well-studied constraint satisfaction problems, tight inapproximability thresholds are
known, often matching the performance of a random assignment. For max-LINSAT in the
formulation considered by DQI, which allows linear constraints with arbitrary acceptance
sets over a finite field, no such worst-case guarantees have previously been established. It
is, therefore, unclear whether the approximation ratios achieved by quantum or classical
heuristics on generic instances are close to being optimal, or whether substantially better
algorithms might exist.
We address this gap in the present work by establishing tight inapproximability results
for max-LINSAT over finite fields. We consider max-LINSAT instances over a finite field1
Fq in which each constraint is satisfied whenever a linear form evaluates to one of r allowed
values, with 1 ≤ r ≤ q − 1. This class includes, as a special case, the linear constraint
satisfaction problems studied in Ref. [6]. A random assignment satisfies each constraint
with probability exactly r/q, yielding a natural baseline approximation ratio.
Our main result shows that this random baseline is provably optimal in the worst case.
Specifically, for every finite field Fq of order q, every 1 ≤ r ≤ q − 1, and every ε > 0,
it is NP-hard to distinguish between instances in which almost all constraints are satisfiable and instances in which no assignment satisfies more than a (r/q + ε)-fraction of
constraints. Assuming P ̸= NP, no polynomial-time classical algorithm can approximate
max-LINSAT beyond the random-assignment threshold, and likewise no polynomial-time
quantum algorithm can do so unless NP ⊆ BQP. From the perspective of quantum optimization, this provides a complexity-theoretic benchmark. The threshold r/q is hence a
hard wall for worst-case max-LINSAT(q, r): no efficient algorithm can consistently exceed
it on arbitrary instances. This does not conflict with DQI’s demonstrated advantage on
OPI, where the Vandermonde structure of classical Reed–Solomon codes enables approximation ratios far above the random baseline. Rather, our result delineates the boundary:
any algorithm that beats r/q on a max-LINSAT instance must exploit structure specific
to that instance. The potential for quantum advantage in this setting lies in leveraging
algebraic structure rather than overcoming worst-case complexity barriers.
The proof builds on and generalizes an inapproximability result of Håstad for maxE3-LIN over finite Abelian groups, which establishes tight hardness for the case of singlevalued acceptance sets [25]. We show how this hardness extends to max-LINSAT instances
with uniform acceptance sets of size r, yielding a matching hardness threshold for the
general problem. The resulting inapproximability bounds are tight.

To establish worst-case limits on the approximation for max-LINSAT, we rely on a
foundational result of Håstad [25, Theorem 5.9], which proves strong inapproximability for
max-E3-LIN-Γ over finite Abelian groups. Håstad’s result builds on the celebrated PCP
theorem [26, 27], which characterizes NP in terms of probabilistically checkable proofs
and underlies most modern inapproximability results. This result provides the base case
from which we extend hardness to max-LINSAT instances with uniform acceptance sets
of arbitrary size.
Theorem 4 (Inapproximability of max-E3-LIN-Γ). For every finite Abelian group Γ and
every ε > 0, it is NP-hard to approximate max-E3-LIN-Γ within a factor |Γ| − ε. Equivalently, max-E3-LIN-Γ is non-approximable beyond the random assignment threshold: it is
NP-hard to distinguish, given an instance of max-E3-LIN-Γ, between
(Y) instances with OPT ≥ (1 − ε) m and
(N) instances with OPT ≤ (1/|Γ| + ε) m.
In particular, setting Γ = (Fq , +) for a finite field Fq yields the (1−ε, 1/q +ε)-hardness
of max-E3-LIN-q. Since each coefficient ai is invertible in Fq , the substitution yi = ai xi
reduces any instance with coefficients to the coefficient-free form (i.e., all coefficients equal
to one) covered by the theorem.
The inapproximability guarantee in Theorem 4 is tight. Indeed, for max-E3-LIN-Γ
over a finite Abelian group Γ, a random assignment satisfies each equation with probability exactly 1/|Γ|. Therefore, any polynomial-time algorithm can trivially achieve an
approximation ratio of 1/|Γ|. Håstad’s theorem shows that it is NP-hard to exceed this

The inapproximability of max-LINSAT(q, r) can also be situated within the broader
theory of constraint satisfaction problems. Under the unique games conjecture (UGC) [28],
Ref. [29] showed that for every constraint satisfaction problem (CSP), the optimal polynomial-time approximation ratio equals the integrality gap of a canonical semi-definite
programming (SDP) relaxation; moreover, this ratio is achieved by an appropriate rounding of the SDP solution. More concretely, Ref. [30] has proven that any predicate whose
satisfying assignments support a balanced pairwise-independent distribution is approximation resistant under the UGC. The max-LINSAT(q, r) predicate for arity k = 3, i.e.,
checking whether xi1 + xi2 + xi3 falls in an r-element subset S ⊆ Fq , satisfies this criterion
(as does the analogous predicate for any fixed k ≥ 3), so UGC-hardness of beating r/q
follows immediately from their result. Our Theorem 5 establishes the same conclusion
under the weaker assumption P ̸= NP, via a direct reduction from Håstad’s theorem [25].
This is notable because the UGC remains open. In a different direction, Ref. [31] proved
unconditional (P ̸= NP) approximation resistance for predicates whose satisfying assignments contain a pairwise-independent subgroup. For prime q, however, the only additive
subgroups of Fq are {0} and Fq itself, so acceptance sets of size 1 ≤ r ≤ q − 1 are never
subgroups, and this criterion does not apply to our setting. For prime power q, nontrivial
additive subgroups do exist, but the criterion still does not cover generic acceptance sets
of arbitrary size r. Ref. [32] extended Håstad’s max-E3-LIN hardness from Abelian to
all finite groups, and Ref. [33] recently transferred these inapproximability results to the
setting of promise constraint satisfaction problems. These results may prove useful for
establishing inapproximability bounds for potential DQI variants over more general finite
groups.

Remark 6 (Tightness of Theorem 5). The threshold r/q in Theorem 5 is tight. Uniformly
chosen assignments x ∈ Fnq satisfy each constraint i with probability exactly |Fi |/q = r/q,

achieving a ratio of at least r/q is obtained by the method of conditional expectations.

4 Discussion
In this work, we have established tight inapproximability results for a family of optimization problems that feature strongly in the decoded quantum interferometry (DQI)
algorithm [6], which has been proposed to offer a speedup over classical approaches. Theorem 5 establishes that no polynomial-time algorithm can approximate the problem maxLINSAT(q, r) beyond the random assignment threshold r/q on worst-case instances, assuming P ̸= NP, and likewise no polynomial-time quantum algorithm can do so unless
NP ⊆ BQP. In light of the fact that this kind of problem is central to DQI, we now
discuss the implications of this result for evaluating quantum optimization algorithms by
resorting to DQI and beyond.
A key observation is that DQI’s quantum advantage claims are not for arbitrary maxLINSAT instances, but for algebraically structured problems, most notably optimal polynomial intersection (OPI), where the constraint matrix has Vandermonde structure arising
from classical Reed–Solomon codes [6]. For OPI with parameters n/q = r/q = 1/2,
DQI achieves an approximation ratio of approximately 0.933 via the semicircle law, while
the best known classical algorithm (Prange’s information set decoding [34]) achieves only
∼0.55 [6]. These insights have recently been extended to algebraic geometry codes in
Ref. [17], where a problem called HOPI is studied, which generalizes OPI. Therefore, DQI
targets structured instances, not worst-case max-LINSAT.
Our worst-case inapproximability result is entirely consistent with these claims: Theorem 5 applies to arbitrary max-LINSAT(q, r) instances, whereas OPI instances possess algebraic structure that DQI exploits via coherent implementation of the Berlekamp–Massey
decoding algorithm. The hardness reduction from max-E3-LIN-q produces instances with
no such exploitable structure.
In DQI, the optimization problem is mapped to a decoding task for a classical errorcorrecting code. A central parameter governing the algorithm’s performance is the decoding
radius ℓ, defined as the maximum number of errors that an efficient decoder can correct.
If a max-LINSAT instance with m constraints arises from a code with decoding radius ℓ,
then DQI coherently maps assignments within Hamming distance ℓ to codewords. The
resulting approximation ratio is given by the semicircle law [6],

It is suggestive to read this connection in coding-theoretic terms. For code families
where efficient decoding beyond a vanishing fraction of errors is believed to be computationally hard – such as generic linear codes specified by arbitrary parity-check matrices, for
which bounded-distance decoding is NP-complete [35] – one would expect the associated
max-LINSAT instances to be similarly hard to approximate beyond r/q, since a sufficiently
good approximate solution would be expected to reveal information about nearby codewords. Making this intuition precise by exhibiting a formal reduction from approximating
code-derived max-LINSAT instances to decoding remains an interesting open question.
Recent work has further clarified that DQI’s advantage, where it exists, depends critically on problem structure: Ref. [16] shows that for MaxCut instances where DQI achieves
any nontrivial approximation guarantee, the optimal solution can be found exactly in
classical polynomial time. DQI consequently provides no advantage for MaxCut. For
unstructured max-k-XOR-SAT instances, Ref. [14] proves that DQI is obstructed by the
overlap gap property, which is a topological barrier in the near-optimal solution space.
Classical approximate message passing outperforms DQI on such instances. From the perspective of computational complexity, Ref. [13] places DQI in BPPNP , thereby ruling out
immediate quantum advantage or “supremacy” arguments of the type as they are used in
quantum random sampling [36, 37]. There, it is #P-hard to compute individual output
probabilities, and an efficient classical sampling algorithm up to a constant error in the
total variation distance would lead to a collapse of the polynomial hierarchy to the third
level. In contrast, DQI is already placed in a low level of the polynomial hierarchy. The
hardness of DQI, if any, arises from locating elements in an exponentially large hidden
subset without group structure and not from the hardness of simulating the quantum circuit itself, as here, in contrast to the situation in quantum random sampling, individual
probabilities of outcomes can be efficiently computed.

Together with Theorem 5, these results rigorously establish that DQI is not a generalpurpose quantum optimizer. Several natural questions remain:
• Inapproximability for OPI and HOPI: Can inapproximability results be extended
to settings studied in OPI [6] and HOPI [17]? The Vandermonde structure of OPI
instances places them in a restricted subclass of max-LINSAT for which generic PCP
reductions do not directly apply, and it remains open whether this structure makes
the problem classically easier or merely differently hard. A positive answer would
strengthen DQI’s advantage claim; a negative answer (efficient classical algorithms
for OPI) would “dequantize” DQI.
• Average-case hardness: Our result is worst-case. Establishing average-case inapproximability for natural distributions over max-LINSAT instances as they occur in
DQI would provide stronger benchmarks for heuristic quantum algorithms.
• Quadratic constraints: Ref. [20] extends DQI to quadratic constraints over finite
fields (max-QUADSAT). For q = 2 with singleton acceptance sets, Håstad [38] establishes tight inapproximability at the 2−d random-assignment threshold for degree-d
equations, giving 1/4 for quadratic constraints, which often appear in practically
relevant optimization problems. Extending this to general Fq and acceptance sets of
size larger than one remains open: the proof of Ref. [38] relies on F2 -specific structure (multilinearity and Reed–Muller minimum weight characterizations) that does
not directly generalize.
• Heterogeneous acceptance sets: Throughout this work (and also in Ref. [6]) the
acceptance sets satisfy |Fi | = r uniformly. If the cardinalities |Fi | are allowed to
vary across constraints, a random assignment satisfies constraint i with probability
|Fi |/q, so the natural baseline is no longer a single reference value but depends
on the distribution of the |Fi |. In particular, instances with small acceptance sets
(|Fi | = 1) are the most restrictive locally, yet mixtures of small and large sets may
exhibit different global approximation behavior. What is a useful notion of a tight
inapproximability threshold in this heterogeneous setting? Should it be governed

Our Theorem 5 provides a complexity-theoretic benchmark for max-LINSAT, establishing r/q as the provable worst-case approximation limit and confirming that any improvement must arise from exploitable algebraic structure. It is our hope that this result
contributes to clarifying the landscape of quantum advantage for optimization problems
of practical relevance [1, 39–41].
